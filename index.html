ChainBench-ADD: A Delivery-Aware Benchmark for Audio Deepfake Detection

Under Review at ACM Multimedia 2026
Code 🤗 Dataset Paper (coming soon)
Abstract

Current audio deepfake benchmarks mainly control the generation side of the problem, while realistic misuse unfolds along delivery chains such as platform re-encoding, telephony transmission, and replay-like capture that can alter forensic cues after synthesis. We introduce ChainBench-ADD, a delivery-aware benchmark that models post-generation delivery as a structured evaluation dimension through delivery families, ordered templates, reusable operators, and realized chains. Built on a matched-parent design linking each delivered sample to a clean bona fide or spoof parent, ChainBench-ADD enables controlled analysis under fixed transcript and speaker conditions. The benchmark contains 941,201 waveforms derived from 55,813 parents and supports five protocol-aware tasks: in-chain detection, operator substitution, parameter perturbation, order swap, and lineage-based robustness. Benchmarking representative detectors shows that realistic delivery remains a substantial source of difficulty, making ChainBench-ADD a useful testbed for delivery-aware audio deepfake detection.

Benchmark Overview

How ChainBench-ADD Works

ChainBench-ADD represents post-generation delivery as a hierarchy rather than a flat list of perturbations. A delivery family defines the coarse scenario (e.g., platform redistribution vs. telephony call). A template defines an ordered route pattern within that scenario. A variant specifies its parameterization, and a delivery chain is the realized path applied to one parent sample. This separation supports analysis at every level—from broad scenario difficulty down to path-wise robustness under accumulated edits.

Benchmark Tasks

Five Protocol-Aware Evaluation Tasks

All tasks are reconstructed from a shared manifest rather than manually curated subsets, together with a leave-one-template-out protocol for cross-template generalization.

1. In-Chain Detection

Metric: EER (%) per family

Bona fide vs. spoof classification within each delivery family. Measures scenario-level difficulty.

2. Operator Substitution

Metrics: Pair Consistency, Both-Correct, NSD

Matched pairs differing by one operator. Tests whether changing operator identity breaks the detector.

3. Parameter Perturbation

Metrics: Pair Consistency, Both-Correct, NSD

Matched pairs differing by one parameter within a fixed operator. Probes within-operator tolerance.

4. Order Swap

Metrics: Pair Consistency, Both-Correct, NSD

Matched pairs differing by one adjacent operator swap. Reveals whether local reordering induces consistent-but-wrong predictions.

5. Delivery Robustness

Metrics: C-FFD, Rk, AURC-chain

Lineage-based evaluation along realistic delivery paths. Tests whether correct decisions persist as edits accumulate.

+ Leave-One-Template-Out

Metric: EER (%) per family

Cross-template generalization. One template excluded at training and evaluated at test time, averaged over held-out templates.

Appendix

Family-Level Rendering Logic

For each parent utterance, the renderer first selects a chain family, then samples a concrete template from that family, and finally instantiates the template-level operators from fixed parameter pools. The direct family contributes a single identity control child, whereas each non-direct family contributes four rendered children. Within a family, template identifiers are deterministically shuffled per parent and sampled without replacement.

For paired templates, one canonical member of the pair is concretized first and its realized operators are then reordered to form the paired counterpart, so that the two children differ only in local order while sharing the same sampled parameters.

FamilyTemplatesChildren/parentRendering Policy
Direct11Always emits the identity control child.
Platform-like64Templates sampled without replacement. No paired-template forcing.
Telephony104Templates sampled without replacement. No paired-template forcing.
Simulated Replay74One paired group (rir_reencode / reencode_rir) may be emitted before ordinary sampling.
Hybrid94Eligible paired groups: rir_aac/aac_rir and rir_reencode_plr/reencode_rir_plr.

A further detail concerns re-encoding. In same mode, the re-encode operator uses the most recent codec already realized earlier in the chain. In cross mode, it differs from that codec and is sampled from {AAC, Opus}. If no earlier codec exists, the renderer falls back to a family-specific default: AAC for simulated replay and Opus for the relevant hybrid templates.

All operators are realized as waveform-domain transforms rather than symbolic metadata tags, and every realized operator is recorded in the manifest trace.

Resampling

Uses ffmpeg with the SoX resampler at precision 28. One-way modes include 16k→8k, 16k→24k, 16k→32k, and their inverses. Round-trip modes are two successive resampling steps with an intermediate waveform.

Band-Limiting

Implemented with ffmpeg filtering. Narrowband: second-order high-pass at 250 Hz and low-pass at 3400 Hz, followed by companding (0.01 s attack, 0.15 s decay). Wideband: second-order high-pass at 50 Hz and low-pass at 7000 Hz.

Codec and Re-encoding

Both perform true encode–decode round trips through ffmpeg. Supported codecs: AAC, Opus, GSM, μ-law PCM, A-law PCM. The only difference: codec follows the template specification directly, while re-encode follows the context-aware same/cross rule.

Packet Loss

Operates on 20 ms waveform frames using a two-state good/bad Markov process:

$$P(\text{bad}\to\text{good}) = \min(1,\; 1/b)$$

$$P(\text{good}\to\text{bad}) = \min\!\left(1,\; \frac{p \cdot P(\text{bad}\to\text{good})}{1-p}\right)$$

where $p$ is the target loss rate (clamped to at most 0.95) and $b$ is the average burst length in frames. Concealment strategies: repeat-fade, interpolation, or noise fill.

Room Impulse Responses

First attempts room simulation with Pyroomacoustics (inverse Sabine absorption, valid source/mic positions, tail truncation, convolution with peak normalization). Falls back to a synthetic RIR generator based on direct path, exponentially decaying random reflections, and a low-level noise floor.

Additive Noise

White: i.i.d. Gaussian; pink: $1/\sqrt{f}$ spectral shaping; brown: cumulative summation; hiss: high-frequency emphasis; hum: 50/100/150 Hz tonal + low-level white; babble: sum of six randomly shifted/scaled streams. For babble, hum, and hiss, a non-stationary amplitude envelope is applied before SNR calibration.

Call Paths

Composite transform with fixed internal pipeline: band filtering → resample to target rate → codec round trip → burst packet loss → jitter-buffer simulation → AGC/limiting. Jitter displaces each frame by a uniform offset in $[-J, J]$, overlap-averages, and trims back to original length.

On the 141,811-sample test split derived from 8,407 parents, we compare each delivered child with its clean parent using off-the-shelf ASR and speaker-embedding backends. Overall: mean ΔWER = 0.061, mean ΔCER = 0.043, mean speaker cosine = 0.941, mean duration ratio = 1.056, mean |ΔRMS| = 2.334 dB.

Notation and Thresholding

Let each sample have score $s_i \in \mathbb{R}$ and binary label $y_i \in \{0,1\}$ ($y_i=1$ for bona fide). Scores are converted to hard decisions by $\hat{y}_i = \mathbb{I}[s_i \ge \tau_{\text{pair}}]$, where $\tau_{\text{pair}} = \tau_{\text{EER}}$ if both classes are present, and $0.5$ otherwise.

Matched-Pair Metrics

For matched pairs $\mathcal{P} = \{(i_m, j_m)\}_{m=1}^{M}$ sharing the same label and differing only in the targeted intervention:

Pair Consistency Rate (PCR):

$$\text{PCR} = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M}\mathbb{I}[\hat{y}_{i_m} = \hat{y}_{j_m}]$$

Pair Joint Accuracy (PJA):

$$\text{PJA} = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M}\mathbb{I}[\hat{y}_{i_m} = y_{i_m} \;\land\; \hat{y}_{j_m} = y_{j_m}]$$

Mean Normalized Score Drift (MNSD): Let $\text{IQR}(S) = Q_{0.75}(S) - Q_{0.25}(S)$. Then:

$$\text{MNSD} = \frac{1}{\max(\text{IQR}(S),\, 10^{-12})} \cdot \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M}|s_{i_m} - s_{j_m}|$$

Symmetric Misclassification Risk (SMR): $\text{SMR} = \frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^{M}\frac{\ell_{i_m}+\ell_{j_m}}{2}$, where $\ell_i = \mathbb{I}[\hat{y}_i \neq y_i]$.

Delivery-Lineage Metrics

A lineage groups samples sharing the same parent, family, and label. Distances are defined on a lineage graph where edges connect realizations differing by one atomic delivery edit.

Censored First-Failure Distance (C-FFD):

$$\tilde{\rho}_g = \begin{cases}\rho_g, & \text{if lineage } g \text{ has at least one failure},\\ D_{\max}+1, & \text{otherwise}\end{cases}$$

$$\text{C-FFD} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{G}|}\sum_{g \in \mathcal{G}}\tilde{\rho}_g$$

Robust-at-Distance $R_k$:

$$R_k = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{G}|}\sum_{g \in \mathcal{G}}\mathbb{I}\!\left[\forall v \in g,\; d_g(v) \le k \Rightarrow c(v)=1\right]$$

AURC-chain:

$$\text{AURC-chain} = \frac{1}{D_{\max}+1}\sum_{k=0}^{D_{\max}} R_k$$

License, Release, and Responsible Use

MIT License

1 License Compliance and Provenance

ChainBench-ADD is built from multiple upstream speech resources and TTS systems with heterogeneous license and usage terms. For this reason, the release distinguishes between assets created by this project and assets obtained from third-party providers.

Assets created by this project include the benchmark organization, metadata manifests, split files, delivery templates, operator configurations, reconstruction scripts, and baseline detection code. These components are provided through the public release.

Upstream speech corpora, model checkpoints, and other third-party components remain subject to their original licenses, model-use agreements, and provider-specific restrictions. The benchmark release should therefore be understood as a release of the benchmark package and its derived annotations and code, not as a blanket relicensing of all upstream resources used during construction. Users are responsible for complying with the terms of the original providers when obtaining, using, or redistributing upstream dependencies.

For transparency, the project documents the provenance of major upstream components, including the speech libraries and TTS systems used during benchmark construction. The documentation also records the software and model versions used in generation and evaluation. If an upstream provider updates its terms or requests changes affecting redistribution, the benchmark maintainers may update the hosted materials, documentation, or access policy accordingly.

2 Release Package

ChainBench-ADD is publicly released as a benchmark for delivery-aware audio deepfake detection. The project website serves as the main entry point and provides links to the dataset release, source code repository, documentation, and benchmark resources.

The released package includes benchmark audio, metadata manifests, split definitions, construction and rendering scripts, configuration files, and baseline training and evaluation code. The metadata support all benchmark protocols, including parent–child linkage, delivery-family labels, template and variant identifiers, ordered operator signatures, and task-specific grouping information. The repository also provides example commands, default hyper-parameters, and instructions for reproducing the benchmark tasks and baseline experiments.

We distinguish between benchmark assets released by this project and third-party upstream assets used during construction. The public release is intended to provide a usable benchmark for evaluation together with a reproducible pipeline. Where an upstream dependency is governed by separate license or access terms, those terms remain in force and are not replaced by the benchmark release itself.

3 Responsible Use

ChainBench-ADD is released for research on audio deepfake detection, robustness evaluation, and benchmark construction. It is not released as a voice-cloning service, an impersonation toolkit, or a turnkey package for deceptive speech generation. The intended use of the benchmark is defensive: to study how post-generation delivery affects detector reliability and to support the development of more robust countermeasures.

The project must not be used for impersonation, fraud, harassment, social engineering, or the creation of deceptive media intended to mislead listeners. The release also preserves and defers to upstream restrictions where they exist, rather than claiming a single unrestricted use policy for all components.

We recognize that speech data may create privacy and identity risks even when obtained from public or permissively distributed resources. For this reason, the project documentation includes provenance information, responsible-use guidance, and contact information for reporting concerns related to licensing, access, or misuse. Future updates to the public release will continue to follow applicable upstream terms and institutional requirements.
